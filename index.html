Some stuff about me

Facts, projects and learnings


Hi, my name is Tim Hagmann and I'm the head of an activity tracking project at Sanitas (a Swiss healthcare company). My team and I are analyzing data from different wearables, apps as well as health care data. The goal is to develop effective nudging mechanisms trough various machine learning methods in order to help Sanitas customers to reach their individual health goals.

I'm holding an economics degree from the University of Zurich and a statistics degree from the University of Neuchâtel. During my studies I did two international exchanges, one to the University of Limerick, Ireland and one to the University of Lille, France. Between my two degree's I spend 9 months (3 months on location) working for the Ethiopian Scout Project (ESP) / SOS Children’s Villages in Ethiopia.

I teach business and economics during Economy Weeks to high school students and I'm currently enrolled in a part-time advanced graduate data science program at Harvard.

Paper: Algorithmic Modelling in the Insurance Industry

Feb 2012 - Jun 2012

The paper examines the classification and regression tree (CART) algorithm as a tool to facilitate the process of model specification needed by GLM when modelling premiums, i.e., to use CART for variable selection, interaction detection and preliminary understanding of the data.

Paper: Earning While Learning - When and How Student Employment is Beneficial

Aug 2009 - Mar 2010

In the paper published in "Labour" we examined how different student employment statuses during tertiary education affect short-term and medium-term labor market returns. Using a representative survey of Swiss graduates of tertiary education, we found significant positive labor market returns of ’earning while learning‘, but only for related student employment and not for unrelated student employment.

Paper:The Economic Consequences of the Irish Famine 1845-1850

Apr 2009 - Sep 2009

The paper examines the short and long run consequences of the Irish Famine of 1845 to 1850. By adopting Krugman’s (1991) two-region model to the Anglo-Irish case, it argued, in line with O'Rourke and other cliometric economists, that the Famine constituted such a shock on the economic fabrique of Ireland that the following Irish industrial backwardness was a result of it.
